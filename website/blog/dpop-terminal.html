DPoP: The End of Token Theft.

OIDC is powerful, but it has a long-standing Achilles' heel: Bearer Tokens. A bearer token is like a 20-dollar bill—whoever holds it, owns it. If an attacker steals your OIDC Access Token from your terminal history or your system swap, they can impersonate you perfectly.

That era is over. Prmana implements RFC 9449 (DPoP) to bring sender-constraint to the SSH terminal.

What is DPoP?
Demonstrating Proof-of-Possession (DPoP) is an IETF standard that binds a token to a specific cryptographic key pair. In Prmana, this key pair is locked inside your machine’s hardware (TPM or Yubikey).

How it Works

When you initiate an SSH session, the Prmana Agent doesn't just send a token. It generates a DPoP Proof—a short-lived JWT signed by your hardware. This proof contains a thumbprint of your public key and is bound to the specific server you are trying to access (via the htu and htm claims).

The server's PAM module then validates two things: 1. Is the OIDC token valid? 2. Does the DPoP proof prove that the sender has the private key bound to that token?

Why it Matters

This is the difference between Probabilistic and Deterministic security. Even if an attacker captures your entire network stream and steals your OIDC token, they cannot use it. Without your physical machine to sign the DPoP proof, the token is worthless dead weight.

Prmana is the first production-grade implementation of DPoP for the Linux terminal. We are setting the standard for what modern infrastructure access should look like.
